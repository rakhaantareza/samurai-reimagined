# 1. How to Use This Document

This document is the single source of truth for all text displayed on the website.

Codex and other development tools should:

- Use the copy in this document rather than inventing new historical content.
- Preserve the intended hierarchy: eyebrow, heading, body, supporting fact, and caption.
- Avoid adding unverified facts.
- Keep body copy concise and readable.
- Maintain the distinction between historical evidence, interpretation, and legend.
- Use the image directions as placeholders until the final asset list is approved.
- Keep editorial notes and source notes out of the visible website.

The visible website copy is written in English because the project is intended for a wider portfolio audience.

# 2. Global Content Rules

## 2.1 Tone of voice

The website should sound:

- Informative
- Reflective
- Cinematic
- Mature
- Historically careful
- Concise
- Human
- Free from exaggerated mythmaking

The website should not sound:

- Like a school textbook
- Like a fantasy game
- Like an anime fan page
- Like a Wikipedia copy
- Overly academic
- Overly dramatic
- Promotional

## 2.2 Writing principles

- Prefer short paragraphs.
- Explain unfamiliar terms on first use.
- Avoid claiming that all samurai followed one universal code.
- Avoid treating the katana as the only important samurai weapon.
- Avoid presenting AI-generated images as authentic historical evidence.
- Avoid presenting legendary accounts as certain fact.
- Use Japanese terms only when they improve understanding.
- Use macrons where practical, such as `shōgun`, `daimyō`, and `bushidō`.

## 2.3 Date style

Use:

- `794–1185`
- `c. 900–1185`
- `1274 and 1281`
- `late twelfth century`

## 2.4 Visible disclosure

> Some visual imagery in this project was generated with AI as an artistic interpretation. It should not be treated as primary historical evidence.

# 6. Introduction — Who Were the Samurai?

## Section ID

`origins`

## Eyebrow

**BEYOND THE LEGEND**

## Heading

**Who Were the Samurai?**

## Opening statement

> The samurai were not a single, unchanging type of warrior. Across almost a thousand years, they served as mounted archers, provincial fighters, retainers, commanders, officials, scholars, and members of a hereditary social class.

## Body copy

> The word *samurai* is linked to the idea of service, while *bushi* more broadly refers to warriors. The two terms are often used interchangeably today, but the people they describe changed significantly across different periods of Japanese history.

> Early warrior families gained influence as the imperial court relied on armed provincial elites to protect land, settle disputes, and enforce authority. Over time, these military networks became powerful enough to challenge court aristocrats and build governments led by warriors.

## Supporting fact label

**THE POPULAR IMAGE**

## Supporting fact copy

> The familiar image of a lone swordsman captures only part of the story. In many early periods, elite samurai were especially associated with mounted archery, while later armies relied heavily on spears, infantry formations, and firearms.

## Pull quote

> “The samurai were shaped as much by administration and political service as by the battlefield.”

This is an original editorial sentence, not a historical quotation.

## Image alt text

**Historically inspired portrait of a mounted samurai archer**

## Source notes

- The Metropolitan Museum of Art, *Samurai*.
- The Metropolitan Museum of Art, *Art of the Samurai: Japanese Arms and Armor, 1156–1868*.
- Modern academic histories of early warrior society.

## Heading

**A History Shaped by Service, Conflict, and Power**

## Introduction

> The samurai did not appear fully formed. Their identity developed through centuries of changing warfare, landholding, political competition, and service to increasingly powerful military leaders.

## Era 01 — Before the Samurai

### Date

**c. 700–900**

### Era label

**FOUNDATIONS**

### Heading

**Before a Warrior Class Took Shape**

### Body copy

> Japan’s early imperial state attempted to organize military service through institutions influenced by continental models. Over time, centralized systems weakened, and the court depended increasingly on regional families with land, horses, weapons, and local influence.

> These provincial elites were not yet the samurai class remembered today, but their armed networks created the conditions from which warrior society later emerged.

### Supporting fact

**Warrior power grew first through practical service: protecting estates, enforcing claims, and managing conflict far from the capital.**

### Image alt text

**Riders crossing a rural landscape in early medieval Japan**

### Editorial caution

Frame this period as a foundation, not as the existence of a fully formed samurai class.

---

## Era 02 — Heian Period

### Date

**794–1185**

### Era label

**THE WARRIOR HOUSES**

### Heading

**Power Moves Beyond the Imperial Court**

### Body copy

> During the Heian period, influential warrior families built regional alliances and served powerful court interests. Among them, the Taira and Minamoto became central rivals in a struggle that reshaped political authority.

> The Genpei War ended in 1185 with the defeat of the Taira. Minamoto no Yoritomo then established a warrior government centered in Kamakura, marking a decisive transfer of power away from the court aristocracy.

### Supporting fact

**Yoritomo became shōgun in 1192, but his military government had already begun taking shape after the Minamoto victory in 1185.**

### Image alt text

**Mounted warriors preparing for battle during the late Heian period**

### Editorial caution

Do not present 1192 as the only uncontested starting point of warrior rule.

---

## Era 03 — Kamakura Period

### Date

**1185–1333**

### Era label

**THE FIRST WARRIOR GOVERNMENT**

### Heading

**Rule Through Loyalty and Land**

### Body copy

> The Kamakura shogunate governed through relationships between the military leadership and its warrior vassals. Service, land rights, and personal loyalty tied retainers to the new political order.

> The period also brought external threat. Mongol-led invasions in 1274 and 1281 tested Japan’s defenses and placed enormous pressure on a system that rewarded warriors with land taken from defeated enemies.

### Supporting fact

**The invasions were repelled, but the cost of defense created political strain because there was little new territory to distribute as reward.**

### Image alt text

**Kamakura-period warriors facing an approaching coastal battle**

---

## Era 04 — Muromachi Period

### Date

**1336–1573**

### Era label

**FRAGMENTED AUTHORITY**

### Heading

**A Shogunate Without Complete Control**

### Body copy

> The Ashikaga shogunate ruled from Kyoto, but its authority depended heavily on regional military governors. Political power became increasingly decentralized as local leaders expanded their independence.

> The Ōnin War of 1467–1477 devastated much of Kyoto and accelerated the breakdown of central control. Its aftermath opened the way to an era of prolonged regional competition.

### Supporting fact

**The collapse was gradual. The shogunate continued to exist after the Ōnin War, but many daimyō increasingly governed and fought on their own terms.**

### Image alt text

**Warriors moving through a damaged medieval Kyoto street**

---

## Era 05 — Sengoku Period

### Date

**c. 1467–1600**

### Era label

**THE WARRING STATES**

### Heading

**An Age Defined by Rival Ambition**

### Body copy

> During the Sengoku period, competing daimyō built armies, fortified castles, managed territories, and fought for survival or expansion. Warfare grew larger, more organized, and increasingly dependent on disciplined infantry.

> Firearms entered Japan in the sixteenth century and were rapidly copied and adapted. The battlefield was no longer shaped only by individual martial skill; logistics, formations, fortifications, and massed weapons became essential.

### Supporting fact

**Portuguese arrivals on Tanegashima in 1543 became closely associated with the introduction and local manufacture of matchlock firearms.**

### Myth correction label

**MYTH TO RECONSIDER**

### Myth correction copy

> Samurai warfare was never frozen in tradition. Successful leaders adopted new technologies and tactics when they offered an advantage.

### Image alt text

**A Sengoku army advancing with spears and matchlock firearms**

---

## Era 06 — Unification

### Date

**1568–1603**

### Era label

**THREE UNIFIERS**

### Heading

**From Endless War to Centralized Rule**

### Body copy

> Oda Nobunaga broke the power of major rivals and challenged institutions that resisted his authority. After his death, Toyotomi Hideyoshi continued the unification process through military campaigns, political agreements, and sweeping control over land and warriors.

> Tokugawa Ieyasu emerged victorious at the Battle of Sekigahara in 1600. Three years later, he became shōgun and established the Tokugawa government.

### Supporting fact

**Nobunaga, Hideyoshi, and Ieyasu are often remembered together as the three great unifiers, although each ruled through different institutions and strategies.**

### Image alt text

**A daimyō overlooking a fortified castle during Japan’s unification**

---

## Era 07 — Edo Period

### Date

**1603–1868**

### Era label

**PEACE TRANSFORMS THE WARRIOR**

### Heading

**When Warriors Became Administrators**

### Body copy

> The Tokugawa shogunate created a durable political order that limited large-scale warfare for more than two centuries. Samurai remained a privileged hereditary class, but many now worked as officials, record keepers, teachers, guards, and administrators.

> Military identity did not disappear, yet everyday life increasingly demanded literacy, bureaucracy, financial management, and ethical reflection. The image of the samurai became more formalized during a period when most samurai rarely experienced open battle.

### Supporting fact

**The long peace helped produce many of the texts and ideals later associated with samurai conduct, even though no single code governed every warrior in every domain.**

### Image alt text

**An Edo-period samurai official writing beside a pair of swords**

---

## Era 08 — Meiji Transformation

### Date

**1868–1877**

### Era label

**THE END OF A CLASS**

### Heading

**A Modern State Leaves the Samurai Behind**

### Body copy

> The Meiji Restoration replaced the Tokugawa political order with a government committed to centralization, industrial development, and military reform. Feudal domains were abolished in 1871, national conscription was introduced in 1873, and the hereditary privileges of the samurai were progressively dismantled.

> A law restricting the public wearing of swords followed in 1876. The Satsuma Rebellion of 1877 became the largest of several former-samurai uprisings and a powerful symbol of the old order’s final defeat.

### Supporting fact

**The samurai did not simply vanish in one moment. Many entered government, education, business, policing, and the new military, carrying parts of their social influence into modern Japan.**

### Image alt text

**Former samurai standing between traditional armor and a modernizing city**

### Source notes

Verify dates against the National Diet Library chronology:

- 1871: abolition of domains and establishment of prefectures.
- 1873: conscription ordinance.
- 1876: law banning the wearing of swords.
- 1877: Satsuma Rebellion.

# 9. Bushidō and Warrior Ethics

## Section ID

`bushido`

## Eyebrow

**ETHICS, DUTY, AND MEMORY**

## Heading

**Bushidō Was Never One Timeless Code**

## Opening statement

> Today, bushidō is often described as a universal code followed by every samurai. History is more complicated.

## Body copy

> Warriors across different eras valued loyalty, reputation, courage, discipline, family interest, and service. But these ideals varied by time, place, domain, and circumstance. They were shaped by military custom, political necessity, Confucian thought, Buddhist traditions, law, and the expectations of individual lords.

> The term *bushidō*, meaning “the way of the warrior,” became especially influential in modern discussions of Japanese identity. Late nineteenth- and early twentieth-century writers helped turn a diverse history of warrior ethics into a more unified national ideal.

## Principle introduction

**Rather than a fixed checklist, these were recurring themes in warrior life.**

## Principle 01 — Service

### Heading

**Duty Was Built on Relationships**

### Copy

> A warrior’s position depended on service to a lord, household, domain, or political authority. Loyalty mattered, but it operated inside systems of reward, obligation, status, and mutual dependence.

## Principle 02 — Reputation

### Heading

**Honor Had Social Consequences**

### Copy

> Reputation affected trust, inheritance, family standing, and political survival. What counted as honorable behavior could change according to period and circumstance.

## Principle 03 — Discipline

### Heading

**Control Was a Practical Skill**

### Copy

> Military training required physical practice, preparation, judgment, and the ability to act under pressure. In peaceful periods, discipline also extended to study, administration, etiquette, and self-management.

## Principle 04 — Courage

### Heading

**Bravery Was Not the Absence of Strategy**

### Copy

> Warrior culture praised courage, but survival, timing, deception, alliance, and retreat were also part of real warfare. Historical samurai were not bound to romantic ideas of constant frontal combat.

## Principle 05 — Adaptation

### Heading

**Tradition Changed to Meet Reality**

### Copy

> Samurai institutions repeatedly adapted to new weapons, new forms of government, changing economic conditions, and long periods of peace. Their history is defined as much by change as by continuity.

## Zen clarification

### Label

**ZEN AND THE SAMURAI**

### Heading

**A Real Connection, Often Overstated**

### Copy

> Some warriors supported Zen institutions or found value in Zen practice, discipline, and attitudes toward death. But not every samurai was a Zen practitioner, and warrior ethics drew from multiple religious and philosophical traditions.

## Pull quote

> “The idea of bushidō became most unified after the world that created the samurai had already begun to disappear.”

This is an original editorial statement.

## Source notes

- Do not present a universal “seven virtues of bushidō” as an uncontested ancient code.
- Note that modern bushidō discourse was shaped significantly in the Meiji period and afterward.
- Use Oleg Benesch’s scholarship and academic work questioning a universal Zen–samurai connection.

**1147–1199**

**late twelfth century, traditional account**

### Role

> Tomoe Gozen appears most famously in *The Tale of the Heike*, where she is described as a formidable mounted warrior associated with Kiso Yoshinaka.

## Figure 03 — Oda Nobunaga

### Lifetime

**1534–1582**

### Role

**Daimyō and first of the three great unifiers**

### Copy

> Nobunaga expanded from central Japan through aggressive warfare, political calculation, and the destruction or submission of powerful rivals.

> He is remembered for disrupting the old balance of power and accelerating unification. His forces made effective use of firearms, fortifications, and disciplined organization, though popular accounts often simplify the details of his tactics.

### Why this figure matters

**He broke institutions that had kept political power divided.**

---

## Figure 04 — Toyotomi Hideyoshi

### Lifetime

**1537–1598**

### Role

**Unifier and ruler of Japan**

### Copy

> Rising from relatively humble origins, Hideyoshi became one of Nobunaga’s most successful commanders and continued the unification of Japan after Nobunaga’s death.

> His rule combined military conquest with land surveys, social controls, and political restructuring. His later campaigns in Korea brought enormous destruction and remain essential to understanding the darker consequences of his ambition.

### Why this figure matters

**His rise showed that military talent and political opportunity could overturn established status.**

### Editorial caution

Do not romanticize the invasions of Korea.

---

## Figure 05 — Tokugawa Ieyasu

### Lifetime

**1543–1616**

### Role

**Founder of the Tokugawa shogunate**

### Copy

> Ieyasu survived decades of shifting alliances and conflict before emerging victorious at Sekigahara in 1600. As shōgun, he established the Tokugawa political order that would govern Japan for more than two and a half centuries.

> His achievement was not simply winning a battle. It was creating institutions capable of controlling daimyō, managing succession, and turning military dominance into long-term stability.

### Why this figure matters

**He converted the victories of the unification era into durable rule.**

---

## Figure 06 — Miyamoto Musashi

### Lifetime

**c. 1584–1645**

### Role

**Swordsman, strategist, and author**

### Copy

> Musashi became famous through accounts of duels, military experience, art, and strategic writing. His *Book of Five Rings* presents combat as a study of timing, perception, adaptability, and disciplined practice.

> Later storytelling turned him into the model of the wandering master swordsman. His historical life and his cultural legend are closely connected, but they are not identical.

### Why this figure matters

**He became the enduring image of individual martial mastery.**

### Editorial caution

Avoid presenting every popular duel story as independently verified fact.

## Figures closing statement

> Together, these lives show that the history of the samurai was never only about combat. It was also about government, social mobility, memory, literature, strategy, and the creation of political order.

# 12. Decline of the Samurai

## Section ID

`decline`

## Eyebrow

**THE LAST TRANSFORMATION**

## Heading

**The End Came Through Reform, Not One Final Duel**

## Opening copy

> The fall of the samurai was not a single battle between tradition and modernity. It was a political, economic, military, and social transformation.

## Body copy

> When the Meiji government centralized power, the domain system that had supported hereditary warrior status was dismantled. Former daimyō surrendered their domains, stipends were restructured, and a national army based on conscription reduced the need for a separate warrior class.

> The 1876 restriction on wearing swords in public became one of the most visible symbols of the change. For many former samurai, however, the deeper crisis was economic and political: their status, income, and traditional place in society were disappearing.

## Mini sequence

### 1871 — Domains abolished

> The feudal domains were replaced by prefectures under central authority.

### 1873 — National conscription

> Military service was reorganized around a national army rather than hereditary warrior status.

### 1876 — Sword-wearing restricted

> The public wearing of swords was banned for most former samurai.

### 1877 — Satsuma Rebellion

> The largest former-samurai revolt ended in defeat against the modern state.

## Closing copy

> The new order defeated samurai rebellion with institutions, taxation, industry, conscription, and modern military organization. Yet many people from samurai families adapted successfully and became officials, officers, educators, entrepreneurs, and intellectuals.

# 13. Cultural Legacy

## Section ID

`legacy`

## Eyebrow

**AFTER THE WARRIOR CLASS**

## Heading

**The Samurai Became an Idea**

## Opening copy

> Once the samurai disappeared as a legal class, their image became even more powerful.

## Body copy

> In modern Japan, warrior history was reinterpreted through education, nationalism, martial arts, literature, theater, and public memory. Ideas associated with bushidō were reshaped for audiences far removed from the society of the medieval warrior.

> Internationally, films, novels, manga, anime, games, and design transformed the samurai into a global symbol. These representations can inspire curiosity, but they also compress centuries of change into a small set of familiar images.

## Legacy themes

### MARTIAL ARTS

> Modern disciplines preserve techniques, forms, etiquette, and philosophies connected in different ways to earlier warrior traditions.

### CINEMA

> Directors used samurai stories to explore authority, violence, loyalty, justice, and social change.

### LITERATURE

> Historical figures became protagonists in biographies, novels, legends, and reinterpretations for new generations.

### GLOBAL CULTURE

> The samurai became one of the world’s most recognizable images of Japan, often blending history with modern invention.

## Myth and history label

**REMEMBER**

## Myth and history copy

> The samurai of popular culture is not false simply because it is modern—but it is not the whole historical story.
